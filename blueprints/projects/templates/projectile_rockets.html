Projectile Rocket Simulation
Replicated calculations for projectile rocket viability

Skills: C++, Computer science, Mathematics, Physics, Python


Introduction

I’ve always been fascinated by space, and especially by space flight. I always like to say that watching the movie October Sky as a child brought me my own Sputnik moment in that it inspired me and made me want to learn everything I possibly could about engineering. Even as I grew and developed other aspirations, I liked to keep up with spaceflight news: to this day, I still watch every SpaceX and NASA launch live whenever I can.

While home from the army for a weekend, I was browsing the news, and a new company, SpinLaunch, caught my eye. SpinLaunch promised to send satellites into orbit almost entirely using projectile motion. It would use a centrifugal arm to spin the rocket payload up to speed before releasing it, allowing it to fly until it neared Low Earth orbit. At that point, a few thrusters would take advantage of the thinned air and easily manoeuvre it into orbit.

I was immediately excited. Rocket engineering is notoriously tricky, as exemplified by the term “rocket science” to mean something exceptionally intellectually demanding; even the physics of a rocket is complex, as when fuel leaves, it applies a reaction force while simultaneously decreasing the rocket’s mass. Projectiles would greatly simplify the modelling problem by reducing the two variables to gravity and drag.

These two variables were still somewhat complicated, however. At those altitudes, gravity has changed a little bit, and drag has changed quite a lot due to lower air density. Therefore, it is no longer accurate to model either as entirely static.

I quickly set to work, trying to replicate SpinLaunch’s calculations. I quickly constructed a differential equation modelling the whole scenario. At first, I attempted to find a solution to the differential equation analytically, but that proved far too complicated, especially for someone whose mathematical brain had been rotting away in a military environment for over a year. I turned to a numerical simulation for a solution. Modelling it first in Python and then in C++ to improve speeds, I managed to replicate the results that SpinLaunch came to.
